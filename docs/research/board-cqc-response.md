# Board CQC Response and Outcomes

This page expands the response material from the [Board Briefing](board-briefing.md). It is intended for the Head of Medical, accountable executive, board medical lead, and any club group handling CQC contact, factual accuracy, remediation, or enforcement risk.

## How to respond to CQC assessment or contact

### Before any CQC contact

The club should already have:

- one accountable executive for CQC/regulatory matters;
- one clinical lead who owns evidence quality;
- a single evidence index and document store;
- up-to-date registered details if any registered service exists;
- legal advice route for registration and enforcement questions;
- communications protocol for players, staff, media, and partners.

### During an assessment

If CQC contacts the club or a registered service:

- establish exactly which legal entity, location, regulated activity, and service model CQC is asking about;
- appoint a single response lead and evidence coordinator;
- preserve clinical records, incident logs, contracts, audit records, and board minutes;
- answer honestly and precisely, distinguishing player-only sports care from public/private healthcare;
- keep a log of requests, documents supplied, meetings, interviews, and actions promised;
- escalate any immediate safety issue to the board and clinical governance lead the same day.

### Draft report and factual accuracy

CQC sends draft reports through its [factual accuracy process](https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-regulation/providers/assessment/factual-accuracy-check/how-use-factual-accuracy-portal) for many assessments. The registered manager, nominated individual, or main partner normally accesses the factual accuracy portal.

Board direction:

- treat factual accuracy as evidence correction, not public relations;
- challenge only points that are factually wrong, incomplete in a materially misleading way, or unsupported by evidence;
- attach supporting documents where relevant;
- keep comments concise and tied to the quality statement or evidence category;
- approve any challenge with legal and clinical review before submission.

## Positive and negative outcomes

### Positive outcome

A positive assessment, or a decision that a service is outside CQC registration, should not be treated as "done." The club should:

- report the outcome to the board;
- preserve the evidence pack and rationale;
- update the statement of purpose and risk register;
- communicate appropriately to players, staff, insurers, and partners;
- schedule annual review and service-change triggers;
- use the result to strengthen RFL, insurer, and stakeholder confidence.

### Requires improvement or negative findings

If CQC identifies concerns, the board should respond with visible control:

- appoint an executive owner for the remediation plan;
- address immediate safety issues first;
- write a corrective action plan with owners, dates, evidence, and board reporting;
- increase clinical audit frequency;
- review staffing, records, medicines, safeguarding, complaints, and contractor controls;
- consider whether the service should pause, narrow scope, or stop accepting public/private patients until safe;
- prepare communications that are accurate, transparent, and consistent with legal advice.

### Enforcement

CQC's enforcement guidance says action should be proportionate to impact and seriousness. CQC may issue warning notices, vary or impose conditions, suspend or cancel registration, prosecute in serious cases, or use other regulatory tools.

If CQC issues a [Warning Notice](https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-regulation/providers/enforcement/warning-notices), the provider can normally make written representations within 10 working days. CQC can set a compliance timescale and will check whether the provider has complied. For other enforcement action, CQC may issue a notice of proposal, allow representations, and then issue a notice of decision that can be appealed to an independent tribunal.

Board direction:

- do not treat enforcement as a medical department issue only;
- form a board sub-group for regulatory response;
- get legal advice immediately;
- separate factual representations from remediation action;
- continue safety improvements even while representations are being considered;
- maintain a clear communications line with CQC, RFL, insurers, players, and staff.

## Recommended initial output pack

The Head of Medical should produce the following for the next board cycle:

1. **Board paper**: the [Board Briefing](board-briefing.md) condensed to 2 to 4 pages.
2. **CQC scope register**: every club medical service, user group, location, and CQC conclusion.
3. **Evidence dashboard**: Safe, Effective, Caring, Responsive, Well-led.
4. **Risk register extract**: top five medical regulatory risks.
5. **Action plan**: gaps, owners, deadlines, board decisions needed.
6. **Appendix**: RFL Medical Standards compliance, contractor due diligence, medicines/anti-doping controls, concussion assurance.

If the club wants to turn this into a repeatable digital process rather than a spreadsheet/document pack, see [Platform Opportunity](platform-opportunity-tool-landscape.md).

## Immediate board decisions

The board should decide:

- whether the club will operate a strict player/participant-only medical service or allow wider private healthcare activity;
- whether any current or planned public, diagnostic, prescribing, injection, remote triage, or contractor service needs legal/CQC review;
- who is the accountable executive for medical governance;
- what quarterly board reporting format will be used;
- whether the club should commission an independent CQC scope review.
