# Board Briefing

## Purpose

This page is a board-facing briefing from the Head of Medical. It explains what the [Care Quality Commission (CQC)](https://www.cqc.org.uk/) is, how CQC assessment works, how it may apply to a professional Rugby League club such as Sheffield Eagles in the Betfred Championship, what evidence the club should hold, and how the club should respond to positive or negative CQC assessment outcomes.

This is not a conclusion that Sheffield Eagles, or any other Championship club, is currently CQC-registerable. It is a governance briefing to help the board understand the boundary between sports medical provision and regulated private healthcare.

## Executive summary for the board

CQC is the independent regulator of health and adult social care in England. It registers providers carrying on regulated activities, assesses quality and safety, publishes ratings and reports for many services, and can take enforcement action where legal requirements are breached.

For a Rugby League club, CQC is most relevant where the club medical service moves beyond participant-only sport care into private healthcare, diagnostics, remote triage, prescribing/injections, public clinic services, or contractor-controlled regulated activity. Core match-day and training-ground care for players can sit within CQC's sports or first-aid exclusions for [Treatment of disease, disorder or injury](https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-regulation/providers/registration/scope-registration/regulated-activities/treatment-disease-disorder-or-injury), but that exclusion should be evidenced, not assumed.

The board should direct the Head of Medical to maintain:

- a documented CQC scope assessment for every medical service;
- a medical service statement of purpose;
- a CQC-style five-domain evidence pack: Safe, Effective, Caring, Responsive, Well-led;
- a contractor due diligence file for external medical, diagnostics, ambulance, and screening providers;
- a response plan for CQC contact, draft reports, factual accuracy review, enforcement, and public communications.

## What CQC is

CQC's functions relevant to a club are:

- **Registration**: deciding whether the legal entity carrying on a regulated activity can provide that activity lawfully.
- **Assessment**: judging quality and safety through the five key questions and quality statements in the [CQC assessment framework](https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-regulation/providers/assessment).
- **Ratings and reports**: for many services, publishing ratings from Outstanding, Good, Requires improvement, and Inadequate. CQC's [levels of ratings](https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-regulation/providers/assessment/assessing-quality-and-performance/levels-ratings) guidance says independent doctors and clinics are rated at service level for each key question and with an aggregated overall service rating.
- **Enforcement**: taking proportionate action where registered providers or managers breach regulations.

The board should treat CQC as both a legal registration risk and a useful benchmark for clinical governance, even where the club concludes its core Rugby League medical service is outside registration.

## How CQC assessments are conducted

CQC's current assessment approach retains the five key questions: Safe, Effective, Caring, Responsive, and Well-led. Under each key question sit quality statements. CQC gathers evidence across six categories: people's experience, staff and leader feedback, partner feedback, observation, processes, and outcomes.

CQC can gather evidence on site and off site. CQC's assessing-quality guidance explains that assessments may be planned or responsive, and that CQC can expand the scope where needed. It can ask providers for evidence, interview staff and leaders, speak with people who use services, observe care and environments, and review records.

For a club, that means an assessment-style review would not be satisfied by policies alone. The club needs working evidence:

- records showing care was actually documented;
- incident logs showing learning and closure;
- equipment checks showing match-day readiness;
- player feedback showing experience and confidentiality;
- contractor reports showing external providers are controlled;
- board minutes showing medical risk is visible and acted on.

See [CQC Assurance Domains and Quality Statements](cqc-assurance-domains-quality-statements.md) for the full framework.

## How this might apply to Rugby League

### Core player care

For a Championship club, core medical provision usually includes match-day cover, training-ground treatment, rehabilitation, concussion management, emergency action plans, mandatory equipment, and return-to-play controls. For example, the [RFL Medical Standards 2026](https://www.rugby-league.com/uploads/docs/Medical%20Standards%202026.pdf) require medical staff, note keeping, emergency action planning, mandatory medical equipment, anti-doping controls, concussion management, and cardiac screening obligations.

That core player/participant care may fall within the sports-ground, gymnasium, first-aid, or temporary sporting-event exclusions in CQC's treatment guidance. The club still needs a written rationale and a governance pack because CQC, insurers, RFL, legal advisers, or the board may ask how that conclusion was reached.

### Does the pitch-side sports exclusion apply to professional sport?

Yes, it can. CQC's current treatment guidance excludes treatment provided in a sports ground or gymnasium, including associated premises, where it is provided for the sole benefit of people taking part in or attending sporting activities and events. It also excludes treatment provided through temporary arrangements for sporting or cultural events. The wording does not say that professional sport is excluded from the exclusion.

The professional status of the competition is therefore not the decisive test. The decisive questions are:

- is the care treatment of disease, disorder or injury?
- is it provided at a sports ground, gymnasium, associated premises, or temporary sporting event?
- is it solely for people taking part in or attending the sporting activity or event?
- is the service only pitch-side/event/player care, or has it become a broader clinic, diagnostic, remote triage, ambulance, or private healthcare service?
- who is directing and controlling the care?

For a professional club, the practical board position should be: **the pitch-side sports exclusion can apply to professional Rugby League, but only within its conditions and only for the relevant regulated activity**. The club should not treat it as a blanket exemption for all medical services.

### Sheffield Eagles / Championship operating context

Using Sheffield Eagles as an illustrative Championship club:

- the club operates in a professional Rugby League environment with RFL medical standards and Championship fixture obligations;
- the club announced in April 2026 that it would relocate to The MEPS International Home of Football Stadium from its Betfred Championship Round 14 fixture against London Broncos on 24 May 2026, which makes venue-specific emergency action planning and treatment-room assurance especially important;
- if the club uses shared premises, temporary arrangements, contractors, or external medical facilities, it should be clear which entity controls medical rooms, equipment, emergency access, records, and provider responsibilities.

The Sheffield-specific governance issue is not "CQC applies because the club is Sheffield Eagles." The issue is whether any medical service model at or around the club crosses a CQC regulated-activity boundary.

### Triggers that need board approval before launch

The board should require formal approval before the club:

- opens a public sports injury clinic;
- treats sponsors, staff families, schools, or community users outside participant/event care;
- offers paid physiotherapy or sports medicine services to non-players;
- runs diagnostic ultrasound, cardiac screening, blood testing, imaging, or remote reporting under club control;
- offers remote injury triage or medical advice beyond normal player follow-up;
- stores or administers controlled drugs or high-risk medicines;
- offers injections, IV products, wellness infusions, or recovery treatments;
- changes event medical, ambulance, diagnostics, or clinic contractors.

Use [Worked Examples](worked-examples.md) and [CQC Registration Edge Cases](cqc-registration-edge-cases.md) for practical scenarios.

## Evidence required to demonstrate CQC-style compliance

The board should ask for an evidence pack organised by the five CQC domains.

| Domain | Evidence the board should expect |
| --- | --- |
| Safe | Emergency action plans, mandatory equipment checks, medicines log, infection-control audit, safeguarding records, concussion incidents, staffing rota, adverse event reviews. |
| Effective | Clinical protocols, RFL compliance, referral pathways, return-to-play outcomes, cardiac screening completion, rehabilitation audit, clinician CPD and scope of practice. |
| Caring | Player consent, confidentiality boundaries, chaperone policy, player feedback, dignity/privacy controls, evidence that clinical judgement is protected from selection pressure. |
| Responsive | Complaints and concerns log, academy/parent communication, loan-player handovers, accessible information, rapid escalation pathways, external provider response times. |
| Well-led | Board medical risk register, clinical governance committee minutes, contractor due diligence, annual CQC scope review, audit action tracker, named accountable executive and clinical governance lead. |

Minimum document set:

- CQC scope assessment for each service;
- medical service statement of purpose;
- legal entity and provider/control analysis;
- RFL Medical Standards compliance dashboard;
- clinical policies and SOPs;
- clinician credential and indemnity register;
- incident, complaint, safeguarding, and duty-of-candour process;
- medicines and anti-doping log;
- contractor due diligence file;
- board assurance report.

The practical templates are in [Assurance Pack Templates](assurance-pack-templates.md).

## How to respond to CQC assessment or contact

### Before any CQC contact

The club should already have:

- one accountable executive for CQC/regulatory matters;
- one clinical lead who owns evidence quality;
- a single evidence index and document store;
- up-to-date registered details if any registered service exists;
- legal advice route for registration and enforcement questions;
- communications protocol for players, staff, media, and partners.

### During an assessment

If CQC contacts the club or a registered service:

- establish exactly which legal entity, location, regulated activity, and service model CQC is asking about;
- appoint a single response lead and evidence coordinator;
- preserve clinical records, incident logs, contracts, audit records, and board minutes;
- answer honestly and precisely, distinguishing player-only sports care from public/private healthcare;
- keep a log of requests, documents supplied, meetings, interviews, and actions promised;
- escalate any immediate safety issue to the board and clinical governance lead the same day.

### Draft report and factual accuracy

CQC sends draft reports through its [factual accuracy process](https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-regulation/providers/assessment/factual-accuracy-check/how-use-factual-accuracy-portal) for many assessments. The registered manager, nominated individual, or main partner normally accesses the factual accuracy portal.

Board direction:

- treat factual accuracy as evidence correction, not public relations;
- challenge only points that are factually wrong, incomplete in a materially misleading way, or unsupported by evidence;
- attach supporting documents where relevant;
- keep comments concise and tied to the quality statement or evidence category;
- approve any challenge with legal and clinical review before submission.

## Positive and negative outcomes

### Positive outcome

A positive assessment, or a decision that a service is outside CQC registration, should not be treated as "done." The club should:

- report the outcome to the board;
- preserve the evidence pack and rationale;
- update the statement of purpose and risk register;
- communicate appropriately to players, staff, insurers, and partners;
- schedule annual review and service-change triggers;
- use the result to strengthen RFL, insurer, and stakeholder confidence.

### Requires improvement or negative findings

If CQC identifies concerns, the board should respond with visible control:

- appoint an executive owner for the remediation plan;
- address immediate safety issues first;
- write a corrective action plan with owners, dates, evidence, and board reporting;
- increase clinical audit frequency;
- review staffing, records, medicines, safeguarding, complaints, and contractor controls;
- consider whether the service should pause, narrow scope, or stop accepting public/private patients until safe;
- prepare communications that are accurate, transparent, and consistent with legal advice.

### Enforcement

CQC's enforcement guidance says action should be proportionate to impact and seriousness. CQC may issue warning notices, vary or impose conditions, suspend or cancel registration, prosecute in serious cases, or use other regulatory tools.

If CQC issues a [Warning Notice](https://www.cqc.org.uk/guidance-regulation/providers/enforcement/warning-notices), the provider can normally make written representations within 10 working days. CQC can set a compliance timescale and will check whether the provider has complied. For other enforcement action, CQC may issue a notice of proposal, allow representations, and then issue a notice of decision that can be appealed to an independent tribunal.

Board direction:

- do not treat enforcement as a medical department issue only;
- form a board sub-group for regulatory response;
- get legal advice immediately;
- separate factual representations from remediation action;
- continue safety improvements even while representations are being considered;
- maintain a clear communications line with CQC, RFL, insurers, players, and staff.

## Recommended initial output pack

The Head of Medical should produce the following for the next board cycle:

1. **Board paper**: this briefing condensed to 2 to 4 pages.
2. **CQC scope register**: every club medical service, user group, location, and CQC conclusion.
3. **Evidence dashboard**: Safe, Effective, Caring, Responsive, Well-led.
4. **Risk register extract**: top five medical regulatory risks.
5. **Action plan**: gaps, owners, deadlines, board decisions needed.
6. **Appendix**: RFL Medical Standards compliance, contractor due diligence, medicines/anti-doping controls, concussion assurance.

## Immediate board decisions

The board should decide:

- whether the club will operate a strict player/participant-only medical service or allow wider private healthcare activity;
- whether any current or planned public, diagnostic, prescribing, injection, remote triage, or contractor service needs legal/CQC review;
- who is the accountable executive for medical governance;
- what quarterly board reporting format will be used;
- whether the club should commission an independent CQC scope review.
