    Private Healthcare Synergies¶

    Why private healthcare is the right comparator¶

    CQC defines independent healthcare as healthcare provided by organisations that are not NHS trusts or NHS GP services. This includes private companies, charities, social enterprises, voluntary and faith-based organisations, and individual providers of care.

    A professional Rugby League club with a medical department is not a typical private clinic, but the comparator becomes strong where the club:

    • provides care outside the immediate participant/event context;
    • charges for services;
    • uses clinical rooms as a treatment site;
    • employs or contracts regulated clinicians;
    • controls diagnostics, records, medicines, referrals, or treatment pathways;
    • treats people who are not contracted players.

    Synergies to borrow¶

    Provider-level accountability¶

    Independent healthcare providers need clear legal-entity accountability. A club should mirror this by naming:

    • the legal entity that owns the medical service;
    • the board member or executive accountable for clinical quality;
    • the clinical governance lead;
    • the registered manager or equivalent day-to-day clinical manager where CQC registration applies;
    • the nominated individual where the provider is an organisation applying for registration.

    Statement of purpose discipline¶

    CQC registration applications require a clear description of services, regulated activities, locations, and service types. Even outside registration, a club should maintain a medical service statement of purpose that defines:

    • who the service is for;
    • what services are and are not provided;
    • locations and mobile/event arrangements;
    • clinical opening hours and emergency cover;
    • referral routes;
    • exclusion boundaries, especially no-public-clinic or no-third-party-treatment rules if relying on sports exclusions.

    Governance evidence pack¶

    Independent healthcare providers are expected to show systems before registration and during inspection. CQC's new provider guidance is useful as a checklist even where a club concludes registration is not required. A club can reuse the same evidence structure:

    • policies: consent, records, medicines, safeguarding, chaperones, infection prevention, complaints, duty of candour, incident reporting, data protection, business continuity;
    • people: professional registration, indemnity, DBS where needed, training, supervision, appraisal, scope of practice;
    • environment: premises risk assessment, equipment inventory, calibration, cleaning, emergency kit checks;
    • care: clinical records, care plans, return-to-play decisions, referral letters, discharge notes;
    • assurance: audits, incidents, complaints, outcomes, player feedback, medical committee minutes, board reports.

    Risk and performance information¶

    CQC's well-led governance quality statement expects leaders to use information about risk, performance, and outcomes. For a club, useful indicators include:

    • serious injuries, head injury assessments, concussions, failed return-to-play steps, and recurrent injuries;
    • emergency transfers and ambulance activations;
    • medicines incidents and controlled-drug discrepancies where applicable;
    • safeguarding referrals and welfare concerns;
    • equipment failures or missing checks;
    • clinical record completion and audit compliance;
    • clinician credential expiry;
    • complaints, concerns, whistleblowing, and duty-of-candour reviews;
    • service pressure, match congestion, travel, and staffing gaps.

    Registration boundary discipline¶

    Independent healthcare providers define their regulated activities, locations, service users, and statement of purpose before operating. A club should borrow that discipline even where its core player-only service is excluded from CQC registration. The biggest private-healthcare synergy is to require a new scope assessment before adding public access, diagnostics, injections, IV services, prescribing pathways, remote triage, or paid clinic services.

    Contracting synergies¶

    Independent healthcare governance is especially useful when the club outsources. Contracts should specify:

    • which entity directs and controls the regulated activity;
    • whether the contractor is CQC registered, exempt, or outside scope, and why;
    • clinician qualifications, professional registration, indemnity, DBS, and CPD requirements;
    • records ownership, access, retention, and subject access handling;
    • incident reporting, safeguarding, complaints, duty of candour, and notification responsibilities;
    • medicines and equipment responsibilities;
    • audit rights and quality reporting cadence;
    • escalation when sporting performance objectives conflict with clinical judgement.

    Cultural synergy¶

    The private healthcare model creates a helpful separation between clinical judgement and commercial or sporting pressure. A club should make explicit that:

    • clinicians owe professional duties to the person receiving care;
    • return-to-play decisions sit with appropriately qualified clinicians;
    • coaches and executives may receive fitness information only within consent, confidentiality, and data protection rules;
    • players can raise concerns without selection or contract retaliation;
    • medical governance reports to the board, not solely through performance staff.
