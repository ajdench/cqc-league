    CQC Registration Edge Cases¶

    Why this page matters¶

    The largest CQC risk for a professional Rugby League club is not ordinary pitch-side first aid. It is service creep: the medical department gradually becomes a private healthcare provider, diagnostic provider, remote triage service, or controller of outsourced regulated activity.

    CQC's current scope of registration is activity-based. The question is not "is this sport?" but:

    1. What regulated activity is being carried on?
    2. Which legal entity directs and controls it?
    3. Is the activity excluded or exempt?
    4. Where is the activity carried on at or from?
    5. Is a registered manager, nominated individual, statement of purpose, location, or controlled-drugs licence needed?

    Edge-case matrix¶

    Scenario CQC registration risk Practical position
    Pitch-side emergency care during a fixture Lower Usually first-aid/event context. Keep emergency action plan, equipment checks, incident records, and onward transfer records.
    Training-ground injury assessment for contracted players Lower to medium Likely sports-ground/gym/associated-premises context if solely for participants. Document the exclusion rationale and keep clinical governance controls.
    Club medical room treats sponsors, staff families, schools, community players, or public paying patients High This looks like private healthcare. Run a formal CQC scope assessment before launch.
    Diagnostic ultrasound, X-ray, MRI referral pathway, cardiac screening, blood tests sent to a lab Medium to high Diagnostic and screening procedures may apply depending on who performs, analyses, reports, and controls the pathway. Use registered providers or register where required.
    Physiotherapist-only standalone treatment Often lower for Treatment of disease, disorder or injury Physiotherapists are not listed for that specific regulated activity, but ultrasound by registered physiotherapists has specific diagnostic exemptions and other activities may still be triggered.
    Club doctor prescribing, administering injections, IV products, or emergency medicines Medium to high Treatment of disease, disorder or injury may apply when prescribing/administering medicines requires a listed healthcare professional, unless the sports exclusion clearly covers the activity. Medicines governance is still required.
    IV hydration or wellness infusions for athletes High CQC treats IV administration of prescription-only/wellness products as potentially registrable treatment when delivered by or under a listed healthcare professional and claimed to alter physiological state. Anti-doping risk is also high.
    Remote injury triage hotline or urgent medical advice service Medium to high CQC regulates remote medical advice/triage where immediate action or prioritisation is provided by a body established for that purpose. Occasional informal advice is different.
    Ambulance or medical vehicle only moves an injured player within the event boundary Lower for transport activity CQC says event-site internal movement is outside the transport regulated activity. Movement from the event ground to hospital can trigger ambulance registration.
    Contractor provides imaging, event medical cover, or clinic sessions at club premises Medium to high The entity with operational policies, staffing, clinical governance, complaints, and quality assurance may need registration. Contracts must identify control.
    Club hosts an independent registered sports medicine clinic Medium Hosting alone does not require the host to register, but unclear responsibility can pull the host into accountability. Use written hosting agreements.
    Academy medical care Medium Sports exclusions may still apply, but safeguarding, consent, records, confidentiality, and parent/guardian communication need stronger controls.

    Treatment of disease, disorder or injury¶

    This is the main activity to test for club medical departments. CQC's Treatment of disease, disorder or injury guidance says it covers treatment by or under the supervision of a listed healthcare professional or a relevant multidisciplinary team, for a disease, disorder, or injury. It includes emergency treatment, ongoing treatment, physical and mental health treatment, vaccinations, immunisations, and palliative care.

    The important sport-specific exclusions are:

    • first aid in unexpected or potentially dangerous situations requiring immediate action;
    • first aid by trained non-healthcare professionals;
    • first aid by organisations established to provide first aid;
    • treatment in a sports ground or gymnasium, including associated premises, solely for people taking part in or attending sporting activities and events;
    • temporary treatment arrangements for sporting or cultural events.

    The exclusion should be documented, not assumed. The more a service resembles a public clinic, commercial treatment service, diagnostics business, or routine healthcare pathway, the weaker the sports-exclusion argument becomes.

    Professional pitch-side care¶

    The sports-ground and temporary sporting-event exclusions can apply to professional sport. CQC's wording is not limited to amateur, volunteer, community, or non-commercial sport. For Rugby League, the right question is not whether the club is professional; it is whether the treatment is:

    • provided in a sports ground, gymnasium, associated premises, or temporary sporting-event arrangement;
    • solely for people taking part in or attending sporting activities and events;
    • confined to the relevant treatment activity rather than a broader healthcare service.

    This means a Championship or Super League club can reasonably document an exclusion rationale for pitch-side care, match-day treatment, and participant-only training-ground care. But that rationale does not automatically cover:

    • a public sports injury clinic;
    • diagnostics or screening carried on as a separate activity;
    • remote triage services;
    • ambulance transport from the event ground to hospital;
    • IV wellness or recovery services;
    • treatment of sponsors, schools, community users, or staff families;
    • an external provider whose own regulated activity is not covered by the club's sports context.

    For board papers, the safest formulation is: the pitch-side sports exclusion can apply to professional Rugby League, but it is conditional and service-specific.

    Diagnostics and screening¶

    Diagnostics are a likely expansion point. CQC's diagnostic and screening procedures guidance includes radiation, ultrasound, MRI, most forms of endoscopy, sample removal or examination for diagnostic reasons, and physiological measurement needed to plan or deliver care.

    Practical implications for clubs:

    • a club that performs musculoskeletal ultrasound as a club-controlled diagnostic service should assess scope carefully;
    • a registered physiotherapist's use of ultrasound has a specific exemption, but this does not automatically exempt a doctor-led or club-controlled diagnostic service;
    • if a third party reports images remotely, that reporting provider may also need registration;
    • if the club sends athletes to an external registered imaging provider, the club should keep referral and result-governance controls rather than duplicating registration unnecessarily;
    • cardiac screening is a governance obligation under the RFL Medical Standards 2026 for some clubs, and the provider/control model must be clear.

    Remote advice and triage¶

    CQC's transport, triage and medical advice provided remotely guidance is narrower than ordinary remote communication. It covers medical advice or triage by telephone or email where immediate attention, action, or prioritisation is needed and where the body is established for that purpose.

    Club risk increases if the medical department offers:

    • a formal injury triage line;
    • an app-based urgent advice service;
    • remote prescribing;
    • immediate-priority decision making for people beyond the club's own players;
    • advice to the public, schools, sponsors, or partner clubs.

    Occasional remote follow-up with a known player may be lower risk, but it still needs records, consent, confidentiality, escalation, and safety-netting.

    Hosted and subcontracted services¶

    CQC distinguishes hosting from carrying on the regulated activity in its legal entity and provider guidance. A host can make facilities available to another provider, but the provider carrying on the regulated activity must register. If responsibilities are unclear, the host can be exposed because it controls the premises or pathway.

    For clubs, contracts should answer:

    • who owns clinical policies and protocols?
    • who recruits, credentials, supervises, and appraises clinicians?
    • whose clinical governance system covers the care?
    • who handles complaints, incidents, safeguarding, records, duty of candour, and notifications?
    • who controls medicines, equipment, diagnostics, and referrals?
    • who pays, invoices, and sets service eligibility?

    If the club keeps clinical governance and day-to-day control, it should not rely on the contractor's registration analysis without checking the position.

    Registration-readiness triggers¶

    Escalate for legal/CQC review before launch if any of these apply:

    • the service treats anyone who is not a player, participant, match official, staff member needing occupational-style advice, or event attendee;
    • the service is advertised externally as a clinic;
    • the club charges fees for medical care;
    • imaging, blood tests, cardiac screening, injections, IV products, minor procedures, or remote triage are added;
    • services are delivered away from the stadium, training ground, gym, or associated premises;
    • the provider/control model is unclear;
    • controlled drugs will be stored or administered;
    • children or academy players are treated outside normal club medical arrangements.

    Use the CQC scope assessment template before approving any of these changes.
