    CQC Governance Baseline¶

    What CQC regulates¶

    CQC registration is activity-based. Under CQC's scope of registration guidance, a provider may need to register if it provides, or intends to provide, health or adult social care activities in England. CQC states that this can apply even where the service is not regular or permanent, and that carrying on a regulated activity without registration is an offence.

    The practical test is:

    1. Is a regulated activity being carried on?
    2. Who is directing and controlling it?
    3. Does an exception or exemption apply?
    4. Where is the activity carried on at or from?
    5. Is a registered manager required?

    For professional sports medical provision, the most relevant regulated activity is usually Treatment of disease, disorder or injury. Depending on the service model, other activities may also matter, especially diagnostic and screening procedures, surgical procedures, transport services, remote triage or medical advice, and any treatment delivered from a clinic not covered by a sports-specific exclusion. The practical boundary cases are developed in CQC Registration Edge Cases.

    Provider, location, and control¶

    CQC registration follows the legal entity that carries on the regulated activity. That may be an individual, partnership, company, charity, voluntary body, NHS body, or other organisation.

    For sports clubs this matters because medical provision often involves several parties:

    • the club operating medical rooms and employing or contracting clinicians;
    • an independent sports medicine clinic providing services at club premises;
    • a governing body setting medical standards but not directing individual care;
    • event medical providers covering fixtures;
    • NHS or private hospitals receiving referrals;
    • contracted imaging, blood testing, rehabilitation, or occupational health providers.

    CQC expects providers to be clear in contracts and service level agreements about who has responsibility for direction and control of regulated activities. If multiple entities carry on a regulated activity, each may need to consider registration separately. For club outsourcing controls, see Private Healthcare Synergies and the contractor due diligence checklist.

    Governance requirements¶

    The main governance anchor is Regulation 17: Good governance. CQC guidance expects providers to have systems and processes to assess, monitor, and improve quality and safety; assess, monitor, and mitigate risk; keep accurate records; maintain staff and management records; and use accurate information to run the service.

    For a club medical service, Regulation 17 translates into:

    • a named accountable executive or board committee for medical quality and safety;
    • a clinical governance lead with authority independent of sporting performance pressure;
    • a risk register covering player welfare, match-day care, medicines, diagnostics, referrals, infection prevention, safeguarding, data protection, emergency response, and contractor oversight;
    • routine clinical audit, incident review, complaints review, and learning actions;
    • contemporaneous clinical records and secure information governance;
    • evidence that concerns are escalated and acted on without delay.

    Fundamental standards most relevant to club medicine¶

    Regulation Relevance to club medical provision
    Regulation 9: Person-centred care Care planning, player preferences, equality, and individual needs.
    Regulation 11: Need for consent Consent for examination, treatment, information sharing, imaging, injections, and return-to-play decisions.
    Regulation 12: Safe care and treatment Risk assessment, medicines, infection prevention, emergency plans, equipment safety, and clinician competence.
    Regulation 13: Safeguarding Children, academy players, vulnerable adults, abuse, coercion, and improper treatment.
    Regulation 15: Premises and equipment Medical rooms, pitch-side equipment, storage, calibration, cleaning, and maintenance.
    Regulation 16: Complaints Accessible complaint routes for players and others receiving care.
    Regulation 17: Good governance Quality assurance, audits, records, risk management, and accountability.
    Regulation 18: Staffing Sufficient suitably qualified, competent, skilled, and experienced staff.
    Regulation 19: Fit and proper persons employed Recruitment checks, professional registration, indemnity, scope of practice, and ongoing fitness.
    Regulation 20: Duty of candour Openness after notifiable safety incidents where CQC-regulated care is being provided.

    CQC assessment lens¶

    CQC's assessment framework asks whether services are safe, effective, caring, responsive, and well-led. These five key questions are the practical assurance domains for registered providers. CQC now assesses them through quality statements, which replace the former key lines of enquiry.

    The most directly relevant quality statement for governance is Governance, management and sustainability, which looks for clear responsibilities, accountability, risk and performance information, robust data arrangements, and use of recognised standards. See CQC Assurance Domains and Quality Statements for the full framework and a sports-club evidence map.

    For sports clubs, the evidence bundle should therefore be built around what CQC would recognise:

    • documented responsibilities and escalation routes;
    • clinical policies and protocols;
    • clinician credentials and CPD records;
    • incident, concussion, safeguarding, and complaint logs;
    • audit schedules and action tracking;
    • medicines and equipment governance;
    • player feedback and staff feedback;
    • contractual oversight of independent providers;
    • board reports that show risk, performance, outcomes, and learning.
